Epic EHR IT Application Controls Review

This case study is the clearest example of how I approach an audit from walkthrough through reporting. The review focused on whether key Epic workflows were supported by controls that were understandable, testable, and credible once evidence was pulled. The file set below is meant to feel like a live engagement, not a presentation-only project.

Scope

  • Epic modules reviewed: Registration, MPI, Orders / Results, Billing, ETL, and reporting.
  • Control types covered: Input, Processing, and Output controls.
  • Objective: validate the accuracy, completeness, and reliability of critical workflows.

What I did

  • Performed walkthroughs with control owners and support teams.
  • Tested selected samples across key controls using logs, approvals, and screenshots.
  • Documented exceptions and linked them to findings and reporting output.

What I found

  • One control exception tied to missing or incomplete approval evidence.
  • One smaller observation where timing or follow-up was inconsistent.
  • Remaining sampled controls operated as expected.
  • Key strength of the file set is the link between testing, exceptions, and reporting.
  • Documentation was intentionally kept plain and direct so a reviewer can follow the story without backtracking.

Method

How I usually approach audit work

I prefer to build the audit story so a reviewer can move from risk to control to testing to conclusion without guessing. That usually means keeping workpapers direct, avoiding inflated language, and making sure the evidence trail is easy to follow. If a control is strong, I say so plainly. If it is weak, I want the file to show why without hiding behind jargon.

What I document early

Scope, control owners, sample basis, evidence sources, and the places where a reviewer is most likely to challenge the file.

What I avoid

Overwritten narratives, weak exception linkage, and findings that sound polished but do not say clearly what happened.

What strong workpapers do

They let another auditor understand the testing path without jumping between tabs or filling in gaps themselves.

What management usually cares about

Whether the issue is real, whether it matters, and whether the remediation path is practical.
